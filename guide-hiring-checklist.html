Drone Pilot Hiring Checklist

Everything you need to verify before booking a drone pilot. From licensing and insurance to portfolio review and safety standards.

1

Licensing & Certification

Legal requirements for commercial drone operation

Valid Commercial Drone License Required

Pilot must hold a valid commercial license for their country of operation: RePL (Australia), Part 107 (USA), PfCO/GVC (UK), or equivalent. Ask to see the actual certificate, not just a claim.

License Current & Not Expired Required

Verify the expiration date on their license. Most licenses require renewal every 1-2 years with continuing education requirements.

Additional Endorsements (If Needed) Recommended

For specialized work: night operations, beyond visual line of sight (BVLOS), or operations over people may require additional certifications.

Company/Operator Registration Required

If applicable, verify they operate under a registered drone operator (ReOC in Australia, CAA registration in UK). This is required for commercial work in many jurisdictions.

2

Insurance Coverage

Protecting your production from liability

Public Liability Insurance Required

Minimum $10 million AUD / $5 million USD coverage recommended for professional work. Higher limits for feature films and high-value productions. Request Certificate of Currency.

Hull/Equipment Insurance Recommended

Covers damage to the drone and camera equipment. Important to ensure the pilot can complete the job even if equipment is damaged.

Your Production as Additional Insured Recommended

For larger productions, request to be named as an additional insured on their policy. This provides direct coverage for your production company.

‚ö†Ô∏è Insurance Warning

Never accept "I have insurance" without seeing the Certificate of Currency. Verify the policy is current, covers commercial operations, and has adequate limits for your production. Some hobbyist policies explicitly exclude commercial work.

3

Experience & Portfolio

Verifying skills and track record

Relevant Showreel/Portfolio Required

Review their work for quality, style, and relevance to your project. Look for shots similar to what you need. A great real estate pilot may not be the right fit for FPV action sequences.

Experience with Your Platform/Camera Recommended

If you need specific equipment (Inspire 3, Heavy Lift with RED, FPV), verify they have hands-on experience with that exact setup.

Similar Project Experience Recommended

Have they worked on similar productions? Feature film experience is different from corporate videos. Ask specifically about projects like yours.

References from Previous Clients Optional

For major productions, consider requesting references. A quick call to a previous production manager can reveal a lot about professionalism and reliability.

4

Equipment & Backup

Ensuring reliable execution on the day

Appropriate Platform for the Job Required

Verify the pilot owns or has access to the right drone for your needs. A Mavic may not be suitable for a Super Bowl commercial.

Backup Aircraft Recommended

For critical shoots, ensure they have a backup drone in case of mechanical issues. Don't lose a shoot day because of equipment failure.

Sufficient Batteries Required

Flight time is limited by batteries. Ensure they have enough batteries for your shoot day. Rule of thumb: minimum 4-6 batteries for a full day.

ND Filters & Accessories Recommended

Professional cinematography requires ND filters for proper exposure control. Ensure they have a full set of filters and any other required accessories.

5

Safety & Operations

Protecting your cast, crew, and production

Site Assessment Capability Required

Professional pilots conduct site assessments to identify hazards, airspace restrictions, and operational requirements before the shoot.

Risk Assessment Documentation Recommended

For larger productions, pilots should provide a written risk assessment. This protects both parties and demonstrates professionalism.

Airspace & Permit Knowledge Required

The pilot should be able to identify if your location requires special permits or airspace authorizations. They should handle these arrangements.

Safety Briefing Protocol Recommended

Ask if they conduct safety briefings for cast and crew. Everyone on set should know what to do when the drone is flying.

Questions to Ask During Screening

Q:

"Can you walk me through your typical safety protocols on set?"

Q:

"What happens if the weather doesn't cooperate on shoot day?"

Q:

"Have you flown this type of shot before? Can you show me examples?"

Q:

"What backup equipment do you bring to a shoot?"

Q:

"Who handles permits and airspace authorization?"

Q:

"Can you send me your Certificate of Currency for insurance?"

üí° Pro Tip: The "What If" Test

Ask the pilot: "What would you do if [scenario]?" Good pilots have clear protocols for equipment failure, sudden weather changes, unexpected aircraft in the area, or someone walking into the shot. Their answers reveal experience level and professionalism.

Red Flags to Watch For

Can't Produce License

"I have one but can't find it" or "It's being renewed" are warning signs.

Vague Insurance Details

Hesitation when asked for Certificate of Currency or unclear coverage limits.

No Relevant Portfolio

Only showing landscape photography when you need commercial work.

Dismissive of Safety

"We'll figure it out on the day" or minimizing permit requirements.

Suspiciously Low Pricing

Well below market rates often means cutting corners on insurance or equipment.

No Backup Equipment

Only one drone and minimal batteries for a full-day commercial shoot.
