About The Speed Registry

Why we created the most accurate and verifiable drone speed record database in the world.

Why We Created This

For years, drone speed claims bounced around the internet with zero verification. YouTube channels claimed "world records" with no GPS data. People sent videos with no witnesses. There was no standard, no transparency, and no way to know what was real.

We built the Speed Registry to change that. This is an official database of verified, documented drone speed records. Every entry has GPS telemetry, video evidence, witness confirmation, and full transparency about how the record was achieved.

This matters because it elevates the entire community. Engineers building faster drones have a clear target. Pilots have verification standards to aspire to. And the world finally has a reliable source of truth about drone capabilities.

Ben Biggs Story

Ben Biggs didn't set out to break the world record. He was an engineer obsessed with one problem: how fast can a custom FPV drone actually go?

"I started building speed drones as a side project. I was fascinated by the physics of it â€” motor efficiency, propeller design, frame weight. Every gram mattered. Every motor choice cascaded through the entire system. It wasn't about being the fastest. It was about understanding the limits."

Over three years, Ben iterated through dozens of configurations. 200 km/h. 300 km/h. 400 km/h. Each time, he documented everything. GPS logs. Multiple video angles. Environmental conditions. He didn't just want to build something fast â€” he wanted proof.

In March 2024, on the Gold Coast of Australia with perfect wind conditions and a witness who understood the technical details, Ben's speed drone hit 661 km/h. Not because he was lucky. Because he'd engineered it down to the millimeter.

The catch? He had the data. He had the video. He had the witness. He had everything needed to prove it. When we launched the Speed Registry, Ben's record became the first entry â€” not because it was the fastest, but because it was the most verified.

History of Drone Speed Records

2016

First FPV Speed Records

Early drone racers began pushing speeds with quadcopters, claiming records of 150+ km/h. No official verification existed â€” just YouTube videos and anecdotal claims.

2018

DJI Claims 179 km/h

DJI's Phantom 4 Pro V2.0 achieved claimed speeds of 179 km/h in tests. Still no independent verification or standardized methodology for speed measurement.

2020

Custom FPV Builds Emerge

Enthusiasts building custom FPV drones began claiming 300+ km/h speeds. The gap between marketing and reality widened. Need for standards became apparent.

2023

550+ km/h Era Begins

Multiple engineers independently claim speeds exceeding 550 km/h. Verification methods still inconsistent. Claims difficult to validate or compare.

2024

DronePro Hub Speed Registry Launches

Ben Biggs' verified 661 km/h record becomes the first official entry. Registry establishes standardized verification methodology. Official source of truth for drone speed records created.

Our Mission

The Speed Registry exists to document, verify, and celebrate the most innovative engineers and pilots in the drone community. We're not here to claim what's fastest â€” we're here to prove it.

Every record in this registry has been independently verified. We don't accept claims. We don't accept estimates. We require GPS telemetry, video evidence, witness statements, and full technical documentation.

This standard benefits everyone. Engineers can build with confidence. Pilots can aspire to records they know are legitimate. Sponsors can invest in innovation knowing records are real. And the community gets honest benchmarks for progress.
